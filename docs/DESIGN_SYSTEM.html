01 — Design Philosophy

Ultimate Dominion's UI evokes a torchlit dungeon — warm amber firelight against deep stone browns. The palette is deliberately constrained: dark earth tones for surfaces, warm parchment for text, and amber for action. This creates an intimate, immersive atmosphere where the world feels permanent and lived-in.

But a text-heavy MUD needs more than warmth. Combat, status effects, item rarity, class identity, and fragment lore all need instant visual recognition. The solution is a small set of signal colors — cool-toned accents that deliberately break the warm palette to draw the eye where it matters.

Core principle: The warm palette is the room. Signal colors are the torches, potions, and spell effects inside it. They should feel like they belong in the world, not bolted on from a different UI.

03 — Signal & Gameplay Colors

These colors intentionally break the warm palette to communicate gameplay state at a glance. In a text-heavy game, color is the primary differentiator between "my character healed" and "the enemy attacked." Each signal color earns its place by serving a distinct, non-overlapping purpose.

Why Fragment Cyan works: The cool cyan (#A8DEFF) deliberately clashes with the warm palette — and that's the point. Fragments are otherworldly lore artifacts. They should feel magical and out-of-place, like something that doesn't belong in a mundane dungeon. The temperature contrast (cool vs warm) instantly signals "this is special, this is different." The same logic applies to Steel Blue for magic — it contrasts with the earthy Copper and Green of physical/nature stats.

11 — Colors in Context

Battle Log

Here's how the signal colors work together in a combat log — the most text-dense area of the game:

Grimjaw attacks with Ashenblade — 28 damage to Cave Troll
Cave Troll retaliates — 14 damage to Grimjaw
Grimjaw casts Arcane Bolt — 19 magic damage
Battle Cry activated! +5 STR for 3 turns
Poison applied — 4 damage per turn for 3 turns
Cave Troll is defeated! +35 gold earned
Lore Fragment discovered — "The Whispering Stones, Part III"

Notice how each line has a clear visual anchor: amber for player, red for enemy/damage, blue for magic, gold for currency, purple for poison/DoT, cyan for fragments. Without these signal colors, the log would be an unreadable wall of tan text.

12 — Audit Corrections

The earlier audit flagged some colors as "inconsistent" that are actually working correctly through the theme's color remapping. Here's the correction:

Theme remaps named colors. The Chakra theme overrides standard color tokens to the Torchlit palette. Using color="yellow", color="green", color="red", bgColor="blue500", borderColor="grey300", and bgColor="grey100" all resolve correctly through the theme — they are NOT using Chakra defaults.

Image Treatment

All game images (items, monsters, portraits) are currently black and white source art. The CSS applies a subtle warm filter via .game-image:

filter: sepia(0.15) brightness(0.95);

This gives the B&W art a faint parchment-warm tone, but the images remain essentially grayscale. This is a deliberate aesthetic choice — it:

  • Keeps the focus on text and gameplay data, not flashy art
  • Evokes old woodcut / engraving illustration style (medieval feel)
  • Makes rarity border colors and stat overlays pop by contrast
  • Avoids the "mobile gacha" look of hyper-saturated item art

Consideration: If images ever move to color art, the sepia filter should be revisited — it would muddy real colors. For B&W source art, the current filter is appropriate. An alternative to explore: slightly increasing the sepia (sepia(0.25)) to make the warm tint more noticeable, giving images more of a "aged parchment illustration" feel.

D. Fragment Cyan — Keep or Warm?

The fragment system uses #A8DEFF — a soft cyan that stands out from the warm palette. Two schools of thought:

Keep Cyan (Recommended)

Lore Fragment discovered

"The Whispering Stones, Part III"

Cool = otherworldly. Fragments ARE mysterious.

Alternative — Warm Gold

Lore Fragment discovered

"The Whispering Stones, Part III"

On-palette, but loses the "special" feeling.

Recommendation: Keep cyan for fragments. It earns its place — fragments are the one truly mystical system in the game. The cool accent makes them feel like something from beyond the dungeon, which is exactly the lore. Just ensure ALL cyan usage uses #A8DEFF (not Chakra's harsher cyan.300).
